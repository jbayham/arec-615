Farm Resource Allocation Problem

A farmer has 500 acres of land available and is deciding how to allocate it between wheat and corn. Each acre of wheat yields a profit of \$200, requires 3 hours of labor, and 4 units of fertilizer. Each acre of corn yields a profit of \$300, requires 4 hours of labor, and 3 units of fertilizer. The farm has at most 1,800 hours of labor available and 2,000 units of fertilizer.

Formulate this situation as a linear programming problem. Clearly define the decision variables, write down the objective function representing total profit, and specify the constraints that capture the land, labor, fertilizer, and nonnegativity restrictions.

Wheat price increase

The price of wheat just increased from \$200 to \$230 per acre. Without running a solver, reason out what happens using duality and complementary slackness.

  1. Write the dual with the new profit vector. For dual variables $y_1,y_2,y_3 \ge 0$ (land, labor, fertilizer), the dual is $$\min\ 500y_1 + 1800y_2 + 2000y_3 \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \begin{aligned} y_1 + 3y_2 + 4y_3 &\ge 230 && \text{(wheat)} \\ y_1 + 4y_2 + 3y_3 &\ge 300 && \text{(corn)} \end{aligned}$$
  2. Prediction: With $c_W$ up to $230$, do you expect wheat to enter the optimal plan? Which constraints are likely to bind?
  3. Find a cheap feasible dual $(y_1,y_2,y_3)$ that satisfies the two “$\ge$” constraints. Can you make one or both constraints bind at equality to lower the dual objective?
  4. Use complementary slackness to recover a primal corner. If your dual has $y_i>0$, the corresponding primal constraint must bind. Solve the implied binding constraints to get $(x_W,x_C)$ and check feasibility.
  5. Compare values. Check that $c^\top x = b^\top y$ at your candidates (strong duality). What changed relative to the pre-shock optimum?
Hint 1 (dual inspection): Try setting one dual variable to zero to cut cost. Which $y$ has a better return? If you try \(y_3=0\) (fertilizer price 0), and force both activities to be produced (so both dual inequalities bind), solve: $$ \begin{aligned} y_1 + 3y_2 &= 230 \\ y_1 + 4y_2 &= 300 \end{aligned} \Rightarrow y_2 = 70,\; y_1 = 20. $$ This gives a feasible dual \((y_1,y_2,y_3)=(20,70,0)\).
Hint 2 (why \(y_2\) dominates \(y_3\) now?): Per unit cost, \(y_2\) “covers” 3–4 units in the dual constraints and costs 1800. The \(y_3\) variable covers 3–4 as well but costs 2000. Setting \(y_3=0\) and using \(y_1,y_2\) tends to be cheaper.
Reveal: A low-cost dual & value With \((y_1,y_2,y_3)=(20,70,0)\), $$ \text{Dual value}=500(20)+1800(70)+2000(0)=136{,}000. $$ Both dual constraints hold at equality for the produced activities (see below), so this is a strong candidate for optimal dual prices.
Recover the primal via complementary slackness Because \(y_1>0\) and \(y_2>0\), the **land** and **labor** constraints must bind in the primal; \(y_3=0\) implies fertilizer can be slack. Solve: $$ \begin{aligned} x_W + x_C &= 500 \quad (\text{land binds})\\ 3x_W + 4x_C &= 1800 \quad (\text{labor binds}) \end{aligned} \Rightarrow x_C=300,\; x_W=200. $$ Check fertilizer: \(4(200)+3(300)=1700\le 2000\). The new profit is $$ 230\cdot 200 + 300\cdot 300 = 136{,}000, $$ matching the dual value (strong duality).
Discussion prompts
  • *Entry:* Why did wheat enter at 230 but not at 200? (Connect to reduced cost / allowable increase.)
  • *Prices:* Why did the labor shadow price fall from 75 to 70 after wheat entered?
  • *Binding set:* Which constraints bind pre-shock vs post-shock, and why does that matter for shadow prices?
  • *Policy:* If labor availability rose by 10 hours post-shock, how much would profit increase, and which constraint binds next?
